How to get Started

To get into chess, the only thing a person really needs is to know how to play. Some call them rules, but considering they're required to play the game, I consider them the 'physics' of chess.

Physics of Chess

A chess board has 16 pieces on each side of the board, with 6 unique pieces that have different physics. Here is a list of the pieces in order of value from least to greatest.

  • Pawn
  • Knight
  • Bishop
  • Rook
  • Queen
  • King

To understand the value of these pieces, these are some definitive movements that differentiate each piece.

  • Pawns
    • The pawns are the pieces that start on the frontlines of the board, and are regularly considered 'cannon-fauter'. If a pawn is making it's first move, it can move two spaces forward instead of one space. Though this is optional and may only be used on the pieces first move. They can only move forward once per turn and only attack diagonally. When taking a piece with a pawn, the pawn will take the place of the original piece. Each side starts with 8 pawns.
  • Knights
    • Knights are the pieces that move unlike any other piece on the board. Their movement is limited to lines that continue straight for 4 spaces and then one space to either their left of right, and cannot move diagonally. The unique aspect of this pieces is that it can 'jump' over other pieces without taking them. The knight can only take a piece if it is in a location where the knight will land. Each side starts with 2 knignts.
  • Bishops
    • A bishop is a piece that starts on the direct sides of the king and queen. Bishops can only move and attack in diagonal directions, but has the ability to move as many spaces as it has avalible. Each side starts with 2 bishops.
  • Rooks
    • The rooks are pieces that start in the corners of a players back row. A rook is very similar in movement to the bishop, in the sense that it can only more in horizontal and vertical directions, but also move as many spaces as it can in one turn. Each side starts with 2 rooks.
  • Queen
    • The queen is the second most valuable piece, and starts next to the king. This piece has the combined abilities of the rook and the bishop, being able to move in any direction and as many spaces as there are avalible. Each side starts with 1 queen.
  • King
    • The king is the most valuable piece on the board. The movement of the king is limited to one space in any direction. The king cannot be taken, rather put in a position where it is in the line of sight of an opponents piece and cannot escape it's current location. If the king is put in check (A state where it is in the range of an opponents piece) the player must use their turn to get the king out of the situation. If the king is trapped with no options to run, fight, or sacrifice another piece, then the opponent declares 'checkmate' and the game is over. Each side has 1 king.

Standard Rules

When playing casually or competitivley, there are some rules that can be enforced if both sides agree that they are necessary. Here are some common rules that can be found among chessplayers;

  1. If a player touches one of their pieces on their turn, they have to move that exact piece.
  2. When playing with a press-timer, if a player does not hit the timer after completing their move, it is still their turn and the other player cannot move until they press the timer.

Forfeiting & Draws

If a player believes that their situation will not get better, and see no positive outcome, the player can knock their king over to declare a forfeit from the game. In which, the other player will declared the victor. Alternativley, if both players find that the game is taking too long or more tedius that it is enjoyable, they can agree on a draw. In this situation, both players forfeit the game, ending without a declared winner.
